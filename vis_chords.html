Chord Progression

While instrumentation varies greatly across music, the big-picture musical structure of a song can be described by its succession of chords.

Unique Chords

This metric is simple: the more chords a song has, the more complex the chord progression. Some songs take a minimalist approach, creating an entire 3+ minute song with only one or two chords, creating variation in other ways. Other songs use not only the seven songs in the key signature, but other "borrowed" chords as well, making the song more complicated.

Looking at the mean and median number of unique chords, it appears as though the number of unique chords has lowered slightly over time, indicating that modern music uses a slightly smaller set of chords on average.

Proportion of chords not in the key signature

A key signature has seven chords that "belong" to that key signature. Including chords that are outside a given key signature adds color and drama to a song. Studying these histograms, we can clearly see that over time, musicians have become less inclined to include out-of-key-signature chords.

Proportion of Songs in a Minor Key

Music in a minor key is often considered sad, and tends to not be used as much in popular music.

Proportion of Songs Where Tonic is the Top Chord

The tonic is the chord that makes the song sound resolved; colloquially, it is the chord that sounds like "home" inside the song. Often, the tonic chord is the same as the key signature we're in, but not always. Music where the tonic is not frequently used have a feeling of being unresolved.

Conclusions

Based on chord-based metrics, it appears as though music has gotten slighty simpler over time.
